Is Traditional SEO Still Worth It for Lawyers?

Every law firm is asking the same question right now. Search is changing fast. Here's an honest answer about what traditional SEO still delivers, what it can't do anymore, and how to build a strategy that works in 2026.

June 22, 2026 14 min read LexScale.ai Editorial

Let's Be Honest: Traditional SEO Still Works — Just Not on Its Own

Google still processes roughly 8.5 billion searches every day. That number hasn't fallen — if anything it's grown. Blue link rankings still drive phone calls at law firms across Ontario and the rest of Canada. Traditional SEO is not dead. But if you've been running the same playbook you used in 2022, you've probably noticed something quietly going wrong.

Here's the honest picture. AI Overviews — Google's AI-generated answer blocks that appear above organic results — are eating the top of the search results page, and they're doing it most aggressively on informational queries. When a potential client types "what is a retainer fee" or "how long does a personal injury claim take in Ontario," Google now answers that question directly on the page. The user gets their answer. They often don't click anything.

Law firms that have built their entire SEO strategy around informational blog content are feeling this hard. Those posts still rank. The traffic just evaporated. It's a disorienting experience — you see the keyword, you see the position, but the phone doesn't ring.

On the other side of the ledger, transactional queries are mostly unaffected. "Personal injury lawyer Toronto." "Divorce lawyer near me." "Employment lawyer Barrie." These searches still produce a local 3-pack and blue link results below the fold. There's no AI Overview sitting on top eating your clicks, because those queries carry commercial intent — and Google has strong incentives (read: ad revenue) to keep that territory looking like traditional search results.

So traditional SEO is alive and earning its keep for high-intent local searches. It's being steadily hollowed out for research queries. That distinction matters enormously for how you should be spending your time and budget.

What Traditional SEO Used to Guarantee (That It No Longer Does)

For most of the last decade, the logic was straightforward: rank on page one, get traffic. A family law firm that ranked #2 for "how long does a divorce take in Ontario" would collect a steady stream of visitors, some percentage of whom would convert to consultations. That logic held from roughly 2010 to 2023. It doesn't hold the same way anymore.

What's changed is the zero-click phenomenon. AI Overviews now answer informational questions directly in the search results. A page that ranked #2 and generated 300 organic visits per month in 2024 might now generate 40. The content hasn't been penalized. The ranking hasn't dropped. Google just placed a full, AI-generated answer between the user and your blue link, and most users stopped scrolling.

This is different from the old Google Featured Snippet problem, which also caused zero-click headaches. AI Overviews are longer, more comprehensive, and harder to "own" in a traditional sense. You can't optimize your way into an AI Overview the same way you could optimize for a featured snippet. The AI synthesizes from multiple sources, and the selection logic is substantially more opaque.

A click-through rate drop on informational content without a corresponding ranking drop is the clearest signal that AI Overviews are cutting into your traffic. Open Google Search Console and compare impressions vs. clicks on your top blog posts over the past 12 months.

The practical implication: traditional SEO's promise of "rank and get traffic" only holds for high-intent, transactional queries now. If you're measuring your SEO by traffic to informational content, you're going to be disappointed — and it's not your agency's fault. The game changed.

What Traditional SEO Still Delivers for Law Firms in 2026

Let's be concrete about what traditional SEO is still reliably doing for law firms, because the obituaries are premature.

Local pack rankings. The Google 3-pack — the map-based results that show three local businesses with ratings, addresses, and phone numbers — is still almost entirely traditional SEO territory. "Personal injury lawyer Barrie" still produces a map pack. Ranking in that 3-pack requires a fully optimized Google Business Profile, consistent citations across directories, and strong local reviews. That's classic local SEO, and it still drives a significant volume of calls for most law firms.

Google Ads amplification. Law firms that run Google Ads alongside strong organic rankings on transactional queries see outsized returns. When both your paid and organic presence appear on the same results page, click-through rates for both improve. Traditional SEO creates the organic floor that makes paid campaigns more efficient.

Technical foundations. Site speed, HTTPS, crawlability, clean URL structure, mobile optimization — all of these traditional technical SEO factors still matter enormously. And they matter for AI systems too. A slow, poorly-structured site will underperform in both Google's traditional index and AI-powered search tools. Technical SEO is foundational infrastructure, not a legacy concern.

Backlinks and domain authority. Links from credible sources — bar associations, legal directories, regional news outlets, law school websites — still drive domain authority. That domain authority influences how your site ranks in traditional search, but it also influences how AI systems perceive your firm's credibility. Backlinks are not a traditional-SEO-only concern; they feed into AI trust signals as well.

The Keywords That Still Behave Like It's 2022

Not all keywords have been affected equally. There's a meaningful category of search queries where traditional SEO is still producing results that look almost identical to 2022 — and this is where your budget should be concentrated.

High-intent, local queries are the safe zone. Things like "employment lawyer Hamilton," "wills and estates lawyer Ottawa," "criminal defence lawyer Mississauga." These searches are explicitly transactional. The user is not asking Google a question — they're looking for a service provider. AI Overviews rarely appear on these queries because there's no informational question to answer. The results look like traditional search: Google Ads at the top, local 3-pack in the middle, organic blue links below.

Practice area plus city combinations are where traditional SEO investment still earns its keep most clearly. A firm that ranks organically for "immigration lawyer Brampton" in position 3 is getting real, measurable call volume from that ranking. The economics of traditional local SEO still work for these terms.

  • High-intent local: "practice area + city" — minimal AI Overview interference
  • "Near me" queries — still dominated by local 3-pack and blue links
  • Competitor-comparison queries: "best DUI lawyer in Toronto" — still mostly blue links
  • Review-seeking queries: "top rated family lawyer Vaughan" — map-pack driven

The strategic message here is simple: focus your traditional SEO budget on transactional, high-intent, city-specific keywords. These are the queries where traditional SEO still converts. Shifting budget away from informational blog content and toward local authority building is the right move for most law firms right now.

Where Traditional SEO Is Fading — And What to Do Instead

Here's where you need to fundamentally change how you think about content ROI. Informational queries are not delivering the same return they used to, and for most law firms, they're not going to recover that return.

Queries like "how long does a divorce take in Ontario," "can I sue my employer for a toxic work environment," or "what happens if you miss a court date in Ontario" are now AI Overview queries. Google synthesizes an answer, displays it at the top of the page, and the majority of users never scroll to the blue links. You can rank #1 for these terms and see almost no traffic.

The strategic shift is this: stop optimizing informational content to rank for clicks, and start building it to be cited as a source in AI answers. That's a meaningfully different objective. Content built for AI citation needs to be authoritative, comprehensive, and structured — not just keyword-optimized. It needs FAQPage schema so AI systems can parse discrete question-and-answer pairs. It needs entity clarity so AI systems understand who your firm is and what you specialize in.

The goal for informational content in 2026 is not to rank for the keyword. It's to be the source the AI cites when it generates the answer. Being cited in an AI Overview — with your firm's name appearing in the source panel — has significant brand value even without a direct click.

This reframe changes your content production calculus. A 600-word blog post optimized for a long-tail informational keyword is mostly dead investment in 2026. A 2,500-word comprehensive guide with FAQPage schema, proper entity markup, and genuine depth has a real chance of being cited by Google's AI, ChatGPT, and Perplexity — which provides brand exposure even without the traditional click.

How to Run Both Strategies Without Doubling Your Budget

The good news is that traditional SEO and AI SEO share the same foundation. You don't need two completely separate programs. The core infrastructure — clean technical setup, strong Google Business Profile, consistent citations, quality backlinks — benefits both. You're not starting from scratch.

What you need is a keyword portfolio lens. Categorize your target keywords into two buckets: transactional (optimize for traditional local SEO) and informational (optimize for AI citation). The effort and success metrics are different for each bucket, and mixing them up is a recipe for frustration.

For your transactional bucket — "practice area + city" terms — invest in Google Business Profile completeness, citation consistency across directories like the Law Society of Ontario listings, Avvo, and Justia, review volume, and on-page optimization for local relevance. Track this bucket by call volume and 3-pack rankings, not organic traffic to blog posts.

For your informational bucket — "how does X work," "what happens if Y," "how long does Z take" — invest in depth and authority. Write comprehensive, expert-level content. Add FAQPage schema. Build topical clusters so AI systems recognize your firm as the authoritative source on a specific practice area. Track this bucket by AI citation frequency (manually test ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity monthly for your target queries) and by brand mentions in AI-generated answers.

  • Your GBP matters for both strategies — keep it fully optimized
  • Your backlinks matter for both — they drive domain authority and AI trust signals
  • Your site speed matters for both — AI crawlers and Google both reward fast, clean sites
  • Schema markup matters for both — structured data helps AI systems parse your content accurately
  • Stop writing short informational posts for clicks — go deep or don't bother

The practical version of this: don't abandon traditional SEO. Evolve it. Concentrate your local SEO budget on transactional terms, upgrade your informational content to genuinely compete for AI citations, and measure the two buckets separately so you're not confusing good performance on one with bad performance on the other.
