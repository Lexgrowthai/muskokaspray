Review Schema for Law Firms: Get Star Ratings in Search

Star ratings in search results are the first thing prospective clients notice. Here's how law firms implement review schema correctly, what rules you must follow, and how to avoid the penalties that trap most firms.

June 23, 2026 11 min read LexScale.ai Editorial

Why Star Ratings in Search Results Matter for Law Firms

When someone searches "family lawyer Barrie" on Google and your listing shows 4.9 stars with 63 reviews directly beneath your title — before they've even clicked — that's a trust signal no amount of copywriting can replicate. The star rating is the first thing a prospective client notices, and in legal services, perceived trust is often the deciding factor between calling one firm versus another.

Review schema is the structured data markup that makes star ratings appear in search results. It tells Google that your page contains review content, what the aggregate rating is, how many reviews that rating is based on, and optionally includes individual reviewer details. When implemented correctly, it produces the yellow stars you see under some search results — a visual element that immediately distinguishes your listing from competitors who don't have it.

Beyond the click-through benefit, review signals are increasingly important for AI search. When ChatGPT, Google Gemini, or Perplexity recommends a law firm in response to a query like "best immigration lawyers in Mississauga," the AI draws on multiple signals — including review volume, rating, and the presence of structured review data on your website. Review schema is part of building the comprehensive credibility picture that AI systems use to evaluate which firms to recommend.

The Two Types of Review Schema Law Firms Need to Know

Schema.org defines several review-related structured data types, but for law firms there are two that matter: Review (for individual client testimonials) and AggregateRating (for the overall star rating). They're often used together.

AggregateRating is what produces the star display in search results. It summarizes a collection of reviews into an overall score. A minimal AggregateRating block looks like this:

"aggregateRating": {
  "@type": "AggregateRating",
  "ratingValue": "4.9",
  "reviewCount": "47",
  "bestRating": "5",
  "worstRating": "1"
}

This block doesn't stand alone — it's nested inside a parent entity, usually a LegalService or LocalBusiness schema block. Google combines the entity description with the aggregate rating to render the full rich result.

Review schema marks up individual testimonials. Each review has an author, a rating, and review text. When you have a testimonials page or individual testimonials embedded in your practice area pages, Review schema tells Google these are real client reviews that can contribute to your ratings picture. Individual reviews are also more useful for AI systems, which can extract specific reviewer statements when generating responses about your firm.

Google's review schema rules require a minimum of 1 review to trigger the AggregateRating display. However, in practice, you'll want at least 5–10 reviews before the rating looks credible in search results. A 5-star rating from 2 reviews raises eyebrows — a 4.8 from 45 reviews builds genuine trust.

Google's Rules for Review Schema — And the Traps That Catch Law Firms

Google has strict policies around review markup, and violating them can result in manual penalties that strip all rich results from your site — not just reviews. Law firms get caught by these rules more often than other businesses because many firms add testimonials to their websites without understanding how the underlying schema interacts with Google's content policies.

Rule 1: Reviews must be collected by your site, not pulled from Google. You cannot take your Google Business Profile reviews and mark them up with review schema on your website. Those reviews belong to Google's platform and were not collected by your site. Review schema on your website must represent reviews that clients submitted directly to your website — through a native review form, a contact form where they share a testimonial, or a third-party review widget that collects reviews on your behalf (not Google's behalf).

Rule 2: No self-reviews. Staff, partners, or the firm itself cannot submit reviews that are then marked up with review schema. Google requires that reviews come from actual clients with a genuine experience of your services. Fake or self-authored reviews in schema are grounds for a manual penalty.

Rule 3: The review content must be visible on the page. Just like FAQ schema, review markup must reflect content that is actually visible to users. If you have a hidden div with testimonials that users can't see, and you mark those up with review schema, Google will flag this as hidden content manipulation.

Rule 4: Don't mark up reviews on your homepage unless reviews are a core feature there. Google generally expects review schema to appear on pages specifically dedicated to a product, service, or business entity — not scattered across all pages as a site-wide rating badge. Your main service page or a dedicated testimonials page are appropriate. Adding review schema to blog posts, contact pages, or your privacy policy page is not.

  • Only mark up reviews your site collected — never Google reviews in your schema
  • Reviews must be from actual clients — no staff or self-authored testimonials
  • Review content must be visible on the page — no hidden markup
  • Don't add review schema to pages where reviews aren't a genuine feature
  • Check Google Search Console's Rich Results report monthly for policy violations

How to Build a Native Review System That Qualifies for Schema

The most compliant and sustainable approach is to build a native client testimonial section on your website where clients submit their feedback directly. This doesn't need to be complex. Many law firm websites have a simple contact form that doubles as a testimonials submission form — "Share your experience with us" — and the submitted testimonials are then published on a dedicated testimonials page or featured on practice area pages.

When you publish these native testimonials, you can mark them up with Review schema. Each testimonial gets its own Review block with the reviewer's first name and initial (for privacy), the rating they gave, the date of the review, and the review text. The sum of these individual reviews feeds into your AggregateRating. As long as the reviews are genuine and were submitted through your site, this is a fully compliant implementation.

The practical process for a law firm:

  • Create a simple testimonials page with a form where past clients can submit their experience
  • After every successful case resolution, send a brief follow-up email inviting the client to share their experience on your website (in addition to Google)
  • Review and publish submitted testimonials — with the client's permission — on your testimonials page
  • Add Review schema markup to each published testimonial and keep your AggregateRating block updated as new reviews come in
  • Validate the schema using Google's Rich Results Test after each update

This process also builds a valuable asset beyond SEO: a page of authentic client testimonials that prospective clients can browse. When someone is deciding between two law firms, a testimonials page with 30 genuine client stories often moves the needle more than any other page on your site. Combining Website Reviews With Your Google Business Profile Strategy

A lot of law firms assume that because they have a strong Google Business Profile with 50+ reviews, they don't need native website reviews. This logic misunderstands how the two systems work and what they each deliver.

Your Google Business Profile reviews appear in the local 3-pack and Knowledge Panel. They're managed entirely through Google and you cannot control them with schema. They're critical for local map pack rankings and are a major factor in how AI systems form local business recommendations. But they appear on Google's platform, not on your website.

Native website reviews with review schema appear as star ratings under your organic search listing — a completely different placement than the local 3-pack. They also add a layer of review content to your website itself, which AI systems can access when they crawl your pages. The two systems are complementary, not redundant.

The best approach is to run both in parallel: actively solicit Google reviews for local 3-pack visibility, and also maintain a native testimonials system on your website for organic listing rich results and AI citation. When prospective clients find your site through both a local pack result (with Google stars) and an organic blue link (with website schema stars), the repeated trust signals compound. Professional Responsibility Considerations for Ontario Law Firms

Law firms in Ontario are subject to the Law Society of Ontario's marketing and advertising rules when publishing testimonials and reviews. While client testimonials are permitted, there are important constraints worth knowing before you build a native review system.

You cannot publish testimonials that include specific case outcomes presented in a way that creates an unrealistic expectation of results — for example, "My lawyer got me $450,000 for my car accident" positioned as a typical result. You must ensure client confidentiality is maintained — publishing a testimonial about a sensitive case (like a family law or criminal matter) requires explicit written consent and careful consideration of what details are appropriate to share publicly. You should obtain written consent from any client whose testimonial you publish, including confirmation of what they've consented to share.

Most client testimonials focus on the experience of working with the firm — responsiveness, communication, professionalism, feeling heard and supported — rather than specific case outcomes. These experiential testimonials are generally permissible and tend to be more persuasive to prospective clients anyway. If you're uncertain about specific testimonials, consult the Law Society's guidelines or seek guidance from a colleague experienced in legal marketing compliance. The full LSO rules on marketing and advertising are available on the Law Society of Ontario's website.
