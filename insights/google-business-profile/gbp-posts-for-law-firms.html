Google Business Profile Posts for Law Firms: What to Post and When

GBP posts are one of the most underused tools in local law firm marketing. They take 5 minutes, they appear directly in your Google listing, and they signal to Google — and Gemini — that your firm is active and engaged. Here's how to use them.

June 23, 2026
11 min read
LexScale.ai Editorial

What GBP Posts Are and Why They Matter for Law Firms

GBP posts appear directly on your Google listing — in search results and Google Maps — when someone looks up your firm or searches for lawyers in your area. They're essentially free real estate on Google's most valuable local marketing surface. Most law firms post nothing. Those that do post consistently get a meaningful edge.

There are four post types available through Google Business Profile. What's New posts are general updates, tips, and news — they expire after 7 days and are the type you'll use most often. Events posts cover upcoming webinars, legal aid clinics, seminars, and community events — they stay visible until the event date passes. Offers are used to promote free consultations or time-limited services and have a custom expiry date you set. Updates are similar to What's New but more persistent — useful for announcing new team members, new practice areas, or office changes. Law firms primarily use What's New and Events in practice.

Why does this matter in 2026? Google's ranking algorithm for local search factors in profile activity. A dormant GBP with old or no posts signals to Google that the business may not be current or engaged. Regular posting signals the opposite. And increasingly, Gemini AI looks at GBP data — including posting activity — when assessing whether a firm is active and engaged enough to recommend to someone asking for a lawyer referral. A firm that posts weekly is, in Google's eyes, a firm that is paying attention.

The competitive picture is stark: the vast majority of Ontario law firms either never post to their GBP or post so rarely that their profiles look dormant. The bar is genuinely low here. A firm that commits to weekly posting immediately differentiates itself from most of the local competition on this dimension alone — and that consistency compounds over months and years.

The 4 Types of GBP Posts and When to Use Each

What's New Posts

This is your workhorse post type. Use it for general updates, legal tips, firm news, and anything time-relevant that doesn't fit a specific event or offer. Examples that work well for Ontario law firms: "Ontario's family law courts are now accepting e-filing for all divorce applications — here's what that means for clients." Or: "Our Barrie office has extended evening hours for client consultations — call us to book." Or simply: "Understanding the 2-year limitation period for personal injury claims in Ontario — miss it and you lose your right to sue." These posts expire after 7 days, which is exactly why posting weekly keeps your profile perpetually active.

Events Posts

Post about actual events your firm is involved in: a legal aid clinic your lawyers are staffing, Free Wills Week participation, a Canadian Bar Association seminar where one of your lawyers is speaking, a community town hall on landlord-tenant rights. Events stay visible until the event date, which means they have a longer window of exposure. Don't manufacture fake events — real community involvement posted authentically is far more valuable for brand and AI trust signals than fabricated content.

Offers Posts

Free 30-minute consultation for personal injury inquiries. Free will kit download. These work particularly well for practice areas where initial consultations are a strong conversion tool — personal injury, family law, wills and estates. One important note: LSO rules prohibit fee-sharing arrangements and require that advertising not be misleading. Free consultations and free information resources are permitted. What you can't do is create offers that misrepresent the value of services or guarantee specific outcomes. "Free consultation to understand your options" is fine. "Free consult — we always get results" is not.

Updates Posts

More persistent than What's New posts. Use these for announcements worth keeping visible longer: a new lawyer joining your team, a new practice area added to your firm, a new office location, a firm name change. Updates are particularly useful for significant developments that you want clients and prospective clients to see for weeks rather than days.

What to Write: 12 Content Ideas for Law Firm GBP Posts

Most law firms stare at the GBP post interface and have no idea what to write. Here are a dozen content categories that consistently work for Ontario law firms at any firm size:

  1. Legal tip of the week relevant to your practice area. Family law: "Did you know Ontario courts default to equal parenting time in custody matters? Here's what that actually means for your situation." Personal injury: "If you've been injured in a slip-and-fall on municipal property in Ontario, there are strict notice deadlines that don't apply to private property cases."
  2. Ontario legal news relevant to your clients. Changes to the Family Law Act, new Landlord and Tenant Board procedures, updates to Statutory Accident Benefits (SABS) for auto accident claims — translate these into plain-language posts your clients actually understand.
  3. Community involvement posts. Sponsoring a local event, volunteering at a community legal clinic, supporting a local food bank or charity drive. These humanize your firm and are exactly the type of content Gemini looks for when assessing whether a firm is actively embedded in its community.
  4. Seasonal legal reminders. Spring: estate planning awareness month. Fall: back-to-school wills reminder for young parents. Winter: slip-and-fall awareness with limitation period reminders. These are easy to plan a year in advance.
  5. Firm achievements. Award wins, speaking at Bar Association events, notable pro bono work, firm anniversaries. These build credibility signals without requiring case-specific information.
  6. New service or practice area announcements. "We're now offering immigration law consultations for families in the Muskoka area — book a free 30-minute call."
  7. Staff spotlights introducing new team members. "We're thrilled to welcome [first name], our new associate focused on family law matters across Simcoe County." Keep these genuine and avoid revealing too much personal information about staff.
  8. FAQ answers to questions you hear weekly. "Can you change lawyers in the middle of a family law case in Ontario? The short answer is yes — here's how that works and what to consider before making that decision."
  9. Local court updates. New e-filing procedures, Zoom court policies, courthouse scheduling changes — these are immediately useful to clients navigating active matters and signal that your firm is current.
  10. Links to your blog articles or guides. Post a compelling excerpt from a recent article with a link to read more. This drives website traffic while keeping your GBP active.
  11. Statutory change alerts. Changes to the Employment Standards Act, Occupational Health and Safety Act, the Family Law Act — translate the change into plain language and explain what it means for your clients.
  12. Holiday and seasonal hours announcements. These are practical and widely appreciated — and they also signal that your firm is actively maintaining its profile.

Keep posts to 150–300 words. Every post should include a call to action: "Call us at [phone] to discuss your situation" or "Book a free consultation at [website]." Posts without a CTA leave potential clients without a clear next step.

What NOT to Post on Your Law Firm GBP

This section is as important as the content ideas above — because the wrong kind of GBP post can create professional responsibility issues under LSO rules, not just marketing problems.

Do not post specific case results that identify clients without their explicit written consent. The LSO's Rules of Professional Conduct impose strict confidentiality obligations on lawyers. Even a positive case outcome cannot be shared publicly without the client's informed, written agreement — and even then, care must be taken not to reveal privileged information.

Do not post anything that constitutes legal advice. GBP posts can contain general legal information — "Ontario's limitation period for most civil claims is 2 years" — but should always include a clear disclaimer that the information is general in nature and that readers should contact a lawyer for advice specific to their situation. The line between information and advice is important in professional responsibility terms.

Do not guarantee results or outcomes. "We fight hard for our clients" is fine. "We guarantee you'll win" violates LSO advertising rules and is also simply false — no outcome in litigation can be guaranteed. Even implicitly outcome-focused advertising ("we always get maximum compensation") risks crossing this line.

LSO advertising rule of thumb for GBP posts: If a post makes a promise about outcomes, reveals client information, or could be mistaken for legal advice, don't post it. Educational, factual, and practice-area relevant content without outcome promises is always the safe lane.

Do not post competitor criticism — this reflects poorly on your firm and is contrary to professional conduct standards. Do not post political content unless it's directly, factually relevant to a legal development (e.g., "Ontario's new landlord-tenant legislation passed third reading — here's what changes"). Do not use stock photos that misrepresent your firm — using photos of courtrooms or lawyers who don't work for you erodes trust and looks inauthentic to both prospective clients and AI systems evaluating your profile.

The Posting Frequency That Actually Moves Rankings

Weekly is the target. Once per week is achievable for most law firms and makes a meaningful difference in GBP activity signals. The technical reason is simple: What's New posts — the type you'll use most — expire after exactly 7 days. If you post once a month, your GBP has zero active posts for approximately 3 out of 4 weeks. To any visitor (or to Google's ranking algorithm) during those three weeks, your profile looks identical to a firm that never posts at all.

The batch writing approach works exceptionally well for busy practices. Sit down once a month — block off 60 to 90 minutes — and write 4 or 5 posts covering the entire month. Then either post them one per week or set a Monday morning calendar reminder to post each one. For Ontario firms, align posts with the Ontario court calendar (fall and spring court terms), seasonal legal themes, and major legal developments as they arise.

Minimum viable frequency: biweekly (every 2 weeks). Below that threshold, you're posting so infrequently that your profile looks dormant for most of the month. Biweekly maintains at least some active presence. Weekly is where the real competitive advantage lives.

On images: GBP posts with images consistently get more clicks and engagement than text-only posts. Use real photos — your office, your team, community events your firm participates in. Avoid generic stock photos, which all look the same and which sophisticated clients can immediately identify as inauthentic. Minimum image size is 720×540px (4:3 ratio). Keep image text minimal — heavy text overlays may be suppressed in some display contexts by Google.

Tracking your post performance is worth the five minutes it takes. Check your GBP Insights monthly: which posts got the most views and clicks? What days of the week perform best? For most Ontario law firms, weekday posts (Tuesday through Thursday) slightly outperform Monday and Friday, but the difference is marginal. Consistency of posting matters more than precise timing.

GBP Posts and AI Search: The 2026 Connection

When Gemini assesses whether to recommend a local law firm in response to a query like "family lawyer in Kitchener" or "personal injury lawyer in Mississauga," one of the signals it considers is how current and active the firm appears. A GBP profile with regular, recent, relevant posts signals to Gemini that the firm is actively engaged with its community and its field. A dormant profile with no recent posts signals the opposite.

This doesn't mean individual GBP posts get injected word-for-word into Gemini's recommendations. What it means is that posting activity is one factor among many that contributes to the overall "active and authoritative" profile that Gemini favours when making recommendations. Think of it as an accumulation of trust signals rather than a single decisive ranking factor.

The practical implication: if you're running a Gemini optimization strategy for your firm, consistent GBP posting should be a standard part of that strategy. Not because each post dramatically changes your Gemini ranking in isolation, but because every signal of activity and authority adds up. A firm posting weekly, getting new reviews monthly, and maintaining a fully optimized GBP profile will outperform a firm doing none of those things over a 6 to 12 month period — particularly in markets where competition for Gemini recommendations is still relatively low compared to traditional SEO competition.

What to post specifically for AI visibility: educational posts that demonstrate expertise in specific Ontario practice areas perform better than generic promotional posts, because they signal subject-matter expertise to AI systems that read GBP content. "As a family lawyer serving clients in Mississauga and the GTA, here are the three questions I hear most often about parenting time arrangements under Ontario's Children's Law Reform Act" is more valuable for AI positioning than "Call us for your legal needs." The former establishes entity signals — who you are, what you do, and where you do it — that AI systems can understand and act on. The latter is noise.
