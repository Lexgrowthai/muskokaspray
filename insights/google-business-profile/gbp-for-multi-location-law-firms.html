Google Business Profile for Multi-Location Law Firms

Multi-location law firms face unique GBP challenges — from duplicate listings to split review equity across offices. Here's how Ontario firms with multiple locations manage GBP at scale without cannibalizing their own rankings.

June 23, 2026
12 min read
LexScale.ai Editorial

Why Multi-Location GBP Is Fundamentally Different

Single-location law firms manage one Google Business Profile listing. They concentrate all of their reviews, all of their optimization effort, and all of their local authority signals into a single point of presence. Multi-location firms face a fundamentally different challenge: they must manage multiple GBP listings, each of which needs to be independently optimized, while maintaining enough consistency across all of them to project a coherent brand — and enough differentiation to avoid self-cannibalization.

The distinction sounds straightforward, but in practice it creates four distinct problems that single-location firms never have to solve.

Review equity dilution. Reviews are split across multiple listings rather than concentrated on one. A firm with 60 reviews across three offices has an average of 20 reviews per location — which is far weaker than a single-location competitor with 60 reviews concentrated in one profile. In local search, Google weights review count heavily in the local pack ranking algorithm. Twenty reviews per location may not break through in a competitive market, even if those same 60 reviews would have dominated the pack as a single listing. Consider a personal injury firm with offices in Toronto, Mississauga, and Hamilton. Each location is competing against established single-office personal injury firms that have spent years accumulating reviews. The multi-location firm needs a disciplined, systematic review acquisition strategy — not just across the firm, but per location.

NAP consistency complexity. NAP — Name, Address, Phone — consistency is a foundational local SEO signal. Every online directory, every citation, every reference to your firm must use the same Name, Address, and Phone format. Single-location firms manage one NAP. Multi-location firms manage multiple NAPs, each of which can drift independently across different directories, legal databases, and website mentions. A family law firm with offices in Ottawa and Kingston, for example, needs to maintain consistent NAP not just on its own website but across FindLaw, Justia, the LSO directory, Google Maps, Apple Maps, Yelp, and dozens of other citation sources — separately for each location.

Category and optimization redundancy. Each location needs to be independently optimized, but the content must be differentiated enough that Google does not treat the multiple listings as near-duplicates. If both your Ottawa and Kingston offices use identical descriptions, identical categories, and identical photos, you are sending weak differentiation signals and potentially triggering Google's duplicate content detection at the local profile level.

Management overhead. Managing three or more GBP listings requires a system. It requires a documented process, a responsible person, a review cadence, and a toolkit. Firms that attempt to manage multiple GBP listings with the same ad hoc attention they gave a single listing will find that listings drift — hours become outdated, review requests stop going to the right location, posts go unpublished for months. Google interprets these gaps as signals of neglect, and neglected profiles rank poorly.

Setting Up Multiple GBP Listings Correctly

The foundational rule for multi-location GBP is simple: one GBP listing per physical office location where clients are regularly served. If you have a physical office in downtown Toronto where clients come for consultations, you can create and maintain a GBP listing for that address. If you have a "paper" office — a virtual address, a shared coworking desk you visit occasionally, or a registered business address you never actually use for client meetings — you generally cannot create a GBP listing for it. Google's guidelines are explicit: GBP listings must represent a location where clients can visit the business during the listed business hours. Attempting to create listings for virtual addresses is a violation of Google's terms of service and can result in all of your listings being suspended.

Naming convention. When you have multiple locations, your GBP listing names need to distinguish them without becoming unwieldy. The two most common formats used by Ontario law firms are:

  • Legal entity + city: "Smith Law Professional Corporation — Toronto" and "Smith Law Professional Corporation — Mississauga"
  • Abbreviated name + office descriptor: "Smith Law PC | Barrie Office" and "Smith Law PC | Orillia Office"

Whichever format you choose, apply it consistently across all locations. Inconsistent naming across your locations looks unprofessional in search results and creates NAP inconsistency issues downstream.

Location-specific requirements. Each location's GBP listing must have its own distinct elements:

  • Its own verified physical address
  • Its own local phone number (not a shared 1-800 or forwarded line)
  • Its own set of hours (which may differ from other locations)
  • Its own unique description, customized for that location's service area and the specific practice areas offered at that office
  • Its own set of photos showing the actual exterior and interior of that specific office

Verification. Each new GBP location must be verified separately. Google's standard verification process requires a postcard, phone call, or video verification for each individual listing. For law firms with 10 or more locations, Google offers a bulk verification option through Google Business Profile Manager. Bulk verification typically requires submitting a spreadsheet of locations with all required data fields, then completing a review process with a Google representative. The process takes longer than individual verification — sometimes several weeks — but it is significantly more efficient for large multi-location operations. Firms planning rapid geographic expansion should apply for bulk verification eligibility early.

Review Strategy Across Multiple Locations

The core challenge with reviews across multiple locations is not getting reviews — it is making sure every 5-star review lands on the correct location's listing. A rave review from a satisfied client in Hamilton that ends up on your Toronto listing does nothing for your Hamilton local pack rankings. It dilutes your Hamilton review count while slightly inflating Toronto's, creating an imbalance that will hurt your multi-location rankings over time.

The solution requires building location awareness into every stage of your review acquisition process.

Use location-specific Google review links. Each GBP listing has a unique short review URL that takes clients directly to the review prompt for that specific location. Generate a unique review link for each of your locations through GBP Manager. Store these links in a shared document that your entire team can access. Label them clearly: "TORONTO REVIEW LINK," "HAMILTON REVIEW LINK," etc. Never share a generic "leave us a Google review" link that forces clients to search for your firm — they may find the wrong location.

Train your team on location-specific review routing. Anyone who interacts with clients — lawyers, legal assistants, paralegals, receptionists — needs to understand which location served this client and which review link to send them. This is not a technology problem; it is a training and process problem. Build it into your matter closing checklist. When a matter closes, the team member responsible for client relations sends the review request with the correct location-specific link.

Track review performance by location monthly. Create a simple tracking spreadsheet: each row is a location, each column is a month. Track review count and average score per location. When a location falls behind, investigate why. Is the team at that location less consistent about requesting reviews? Is the client experience there weaker? Is the intake volume lower? Understanding the cause allows you to address it specifically.

Handle review imbalance proactively. If one location significantly outperforms others in review count, do not simply coast on that lead — focus acquisition effort on the weaker locations. A firm where the Toronto office has 85 reviews and the Mississauga office has 12 reviews is leaving significant local ranking potential on the table in the Mississauga market. Local pack rankings for "family lawyer Mississauga" are determined by the Mississauga listing's signals, not by the Toronto listing's dominance.

Consider a location-stamped review request email template. For centralized intake models — where all client intake flows through a central team — track which office will handle the matter and route review requests accordingly at matter close. A simple field in your matter management system ("serving office") enables this routing without any complex technology.

Keeping NAP Consistent Across All Locations

NAP consistency is the foundation of local SEO, and for multi-location firms it is the most labor-intensive ongoing requirement. Every online reference to each of your locations needs to use exactly the same Name, Address, and Phone format. "Suite 400" and "#400" are different. "Professional Corporation" and "PC" are different. "647-555-1234" and "(647) 555-1234" are different. These variations may seem trivial, but local search algorithms treat inconsistent NAP as a confidence-reducing signal — it suggests the listings may represent different entities or that the business data is unreliable.

Step 1: Create a master NAP document. Before doing any citation work, create a master NAP record for each location. This is the canonical, official version of each location's Name, Address, and Phone. Every future citation must match this record exactly. Include: legal business name (as it appears on your GBP), full street address with suite/unit formatted consistently, city, province, postal code, and local phone number. Store this document somewhere your team can always reference it.

Step 2: Audit every online mention of each location. The key citation sources for Ontario law firms include: Google Maps (your GBP itself), Apple Maps, Yelp, FindLaw, Justia, the Law Society of Ontario directory, your own website's contact page and location pages, legal directories like Lawyer.com and Avvo, and any local business directories like Yellow Pages Canada and Canada411. Check each location's listing on each of these sources against your master NAP document. Note every discrepancy.

Step 3: Correct inconsistencies systematically. Many citation sources allow business owners to claim and edit their listings. The LSO directory is an important one for Ontario law firms — ensuring your office address in the LSO directory exactly matches your GBP is a high-priority task. For broad-scale citation correction, consider using a citation management tool. BrightLocal and Whitespark both have strong coverage of Canadian citation sources. Yext is an option but tends to be more US-focused. For Ontario firms specifically, Whitespark's Canadian citation building service is particularly relevant.

The call tracking challenge. Many law firms use call tracking numbers for their advertising — unique phone numbers assigned to specific campaigns that enable attribution of inbound calls to specific ads or sources. Call tracking numbers create a direct conflict with NAP consistency: if you publish a call tracking number on your GBP listing, it differs from the number on your website and directories, creating an inconsistency. The standard solution is to use call tracking numbers only on your website and in paid advertising, never on your GBP listings. Your GBP should always show your permanent, consistent local phone number. If you need call tracking on GBP specifically, use Google's call history feature within GBP rather than an external call tracking number.

Content Strategy for Multi-Location GBP Profiles

The risk multi-location firms face with content is over-standardization: using the same description, the same categories, and the same template across all locations. When every location's profile looks identical, you are missing the opportunity to rank for location-specific searches, and you risk Google treating your listings as near-duplicates.

Location-specific descriptions. Each location's GBP description should be unique, locally relevant, and specifically optimized for the practice areas most prominent at that office. A firm with a Toronto Bay Street office that focuses on corporate and commercial law and a Hamilton office that focuses on personal injury and family law should have completely different descriptions for each location. Even if both offices offer the same full-service practice, lean into the geographic context: reference the specific city, reference the local courthouse if relevant, mention local community involvement specific to that office. Use all 750 characters available in the description field.

Location-specific photos. Google weights photo content when evaluating GBP profile completeness. Each location's profile should include: exterior photos of that specific building (including streetscape context), interior photos of that specific office (reception area, boardroom, consultation rooms), and team photos specific to the lawyers based at that location. Do not reuse photos from one location on another location's profile. Google can often detect duplicate imagery, and even if it cannot, clients who visit the office after seeing photos expect the photos to match what they see.

Location-specific posts. GBP posts — the short updates you can publish to your profile — are an opportunity to differentiate your locations through local relevance. Reference local events in posts for each specific location. A Hamilton office might post about a local community legal clinic it participated in. An Ottawa office might reference national capital-specific legal news. Posts that mention the city name explicitly reinforce geographic relevance signals for that location.

Location-specific landing pages on your website. Each GBP location should link to a dedicated landing page on your website — not just your homepage. The location-specific landing page should include the full NAP for that office, a description of the services offered at that location, the local community context, a Google Maps embed for that specific location, and LocalBusiness JSON-LD schema markup with the location-specific data. This creates a reinforcing loop: Google's local ranking algorithm cross-references GBP data with website signals. When your GBP and your location page agree on address, phone, practice areas, and service geography, your local rankings for that specific market improve.

Managing Multiple Locations at Scale

The operational challenge of multi-location GBP management is real. Firms that do not build a system for it will find their listings drifting — and drift costs rankings. Here is how to build a system that scales.

GBP Business Manager account structure. All of your locations should be managed through a single Google Business Profile Manager account. The primary owner of this account should be a firm-controlled email address — not a personal Gmail belonging to a specific employee. Use an address like gbp@yourfirm.ca or marketing@yourfirm.ca. When you use a personal email as primary owner and that employee leaves the firm, you can lose access to all of your GBP listings simultaneously. This is one of the most damaging operational failures we see at multi-location firms. Add individual team members as managers rather than owners, so that their access can be revoked without affecting the primary account ownership.

Bulk management tools. For firms with three or more locations, manual management through the GBP interface becomes inefficient. Tools worth evaluating include:

  • BrightLocal — strong reporting, review monitoring, and citation management for Canadian markets
  • Whitespark — particularly strong for Canadian citation building; also offers GBP management features
  • Yext — enterprise-scale listing management; expensive but powerful for firms with 10+ locations

Monthly GBP audit checklist. Every month, run a quick audit across all your GBP locations. The checklist should cover:

  • Pending suggested edits — Google allows anyone to suggest changes to your business information. These suggestions can appear on your listing if you do not review and reject them. Check monthly.
  • Review count and score changes — track by location and flag any unusual patterns (sudden drop in score, no new reviews in 30 days)
  • Spam or fake reviews — check for new reviews that appear to be from competitors or bad actors; flag for removal
  • Photo count — each location should have at least 15 photos; add new photos quarterly
  • Most recent post date — no location should go more than 4 weeks without a GBP post
  • Hours accuracy — particularly important around holidays and if any location has changed its hours

Performance tracking by location. GBP provides performance data including calls, website clicks, and direction requests. Track these metrics separately per location, monthly. This data helps you identify which locations are underperforming in GBP-driven leads and focus optimization effort accordingly. A location with high direction requests but low calls may have a phone conversion problem. A location with low direction requests relative to search impressions may have a local pack visibility problem. The metrics tell you where to focus.
