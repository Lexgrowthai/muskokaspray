Using Google Gemini to Write Law Firm Content

Gemini can speed up content creation for law firms — but using it the wrong way will cost you. Here's an honest guide to what it's actually good for and where you still need a human.

11 min read
Updated June 2026
LexScale.ai Editorial

What Gemini Is Actually Good At for Law Firm Content

Let's be direct about this: Google Gemini is a genuinely useful tool for law firm content in specific, well-defined situations. The mistake most firms make isn't using AI — it's either expecting too much from it or using it without any human review process at all. Understanding the actual strengths of Gemini helps you build a content workflow that produces real results.

Gemini is excellent at structural tasks. If you need an outline for a blog post on Ontario separation agreements, Gemini can produce a solid framework in seconds. It understands how legal content is typically organized — introduction, key concepts, practical implications, FAQ — and it can apply that structure across dozens of topics without losing consistency. For a Toronto family law firm that needs to publish regularly, that alone is a time-saver worth having.

The platform is also surprisingly good at generating FAQ questions. Clients ask remarkably similar questions across practice areas, and Gemini has absorbed enough of the internet's legal Q&A to know what those questions are. A family lawyer in Mississauga can ask Gemini to generate 20 common questions about separation agreements, get a usable list in thirty seconds, and then write the answers themselves. That process is faster and better than trying to brainstorm questions from scratch. If you're building toward Gemini SEO for lawyers, FAQ content is one of the most important signals you can build.

Content repurposing is another strong suit. If your firm has published a 2,000-word blog post on parenting time disputes, Gemini can quickly transform it into a shorter FAQ version, a social media thread, an email newsletter intro, and a set of meta descriptions. The raw material is your original content — Gemini just reshapes it. This kind of repurposing is low-risk because you're not asking Gemini to generate new legal facts; you're asking it to restructure information you've already verified.

Consider what this looks like in practice. A Toronto family law firm that needs 40 FAQ answers about separation agreements would traditionally spend a full week drafting those questions and answers. Using Gemini to generate rough drafts of all 40 in an afternoon — then having a lawyer spend two days reviewing and correcting each answer — cuts the total time by more than half while maintaining the legal accuracy the content needs. The value is in the velocity, not in treating the output as finished.

Where Gemini Falls Down for Legal Writing

The risks of using Gemini for law firm content are real and specific. This isn't a general warning about AI being imperfect — it's about the particular ways that AI failure intersects with the professional and reputational stakes of legal practice in Ontario.

The most serious problem is confident inaccuracy. Gemini doesn't hedge when it gets things wrong. It will state a limitation period under Ontario's Limitations Act, 2002 with the same fluency whether it's correct or not. It will cite an LSO rule that doesn't exist, describe a court process that has changed, or summarize a statute that has been amended — and there's nothing in the output to signal that something might be wrong. For a law firm, this isn't a minor inconvenience; it's a professional conduct issue. Ontario's Rules of Professional Conduct require accuracy in all marketing materials, including website content and blog posts.

RISK
Gemini regularly hallucinates legal facts with complete confidence. In testing, it has cited incorrect limitation periods under Ontario's Limitations Act, 2002, misrepresented LSO rules, and described court processes that don't exist in Ontario. Publishing this content unreviewed creates both professional conduct exposure and client trust damage that is very difficult to recover from.

The second category of failure is depth. Legal writing — the kind that earns trust from both clients and search engines — comes from experience. When a criminal defence lawyer in Brampton writes about what happens after an arrest, the most valuable content comes from what they've actually seen in Brampton courts: what judges tend to do, how duty counsel operates at the specific courthouse, what the actual timelines look like. Gemini cannot produce this. It can write generic content about the arrest process. It cannot write content that reflects what it means to have appeared at College Park courthouse 200 times.

This matters enormously for ranking in Google Gemini. The E-E-A-T signals Google evaluates — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness — are specifically designed to reward content that reflects real-world knowledge. Generic AI content that hasn't been enriched with genuine legal expertise will underperform on these signals, regardless of how well it's structured.

Gemini also lacks access to current information about Ontario-specific legal developments. A law change that happened last month, a significant Ontario Court of Appeal decision from this quarter, a new LSO rule — Gemini's training data has a cutoff date and it doesn't know what it doesn't know. Content about areas of law that have changed recently is particularly dangerous to publish without careful lawyer review.

The Editing Workflow That Makes Gemini Content Publishable

The difference between law firms that successfully use Gemini for content and those that create problems for themselves is almost entirely about the editing workflow that follows the AI output. A five-step process covers the critical checkpoints.

WORKFLOW TIP
The most effective Gemini content workflow treats AI output as a first draft from a very fast but non-specialist junior writer. You wouldn't publish a junior's first draft without review — apply the same standard to Gemini. The review process should take at minimum as long as writing the first draft would have taken without AI assistance.

Step 1: Brief Gemini with specific Ontario context. Generic prompts produce generic output. Before you ask Gemini to write anything, tell it the jurisdiction (Ontario), the practice area, the specific audience (clients facing a separation, small business owners considering incorporation, someone who has just been charged with impaired driving), and any Ontario-specific constraints. A prompt that begins "Write a blog post about divorce" will produce far weaker content than one that begins "Write a blog post for an Ontario family law firm explaining the difference between divorce and legal separation under the Divorce Act and the Family Law Act, written for a client in the early stages of considering separation in Toronto."

Step 2: Generate a raw draft and treat it as a starting point. Read the output with healthy skepticism. Don't look for what to cut — look for what needs to be corrected, deepened, or verified. Flag every specific legal claim, every statute reference, every procedural description, and every time frame before you move on.

Step 3: Lawyer review for legal accuracy. Every statute reference, limitation period, and procedural claim must be verified against primary sources. If Gemini writes that the basic limitation period in Ontario is two years, verify it against the actual Limitations Act, 2002, s. 4. If it describes the parenting time provisions under the Children's Law Reform Act, verify the current version of those provisions on ontario.ca. This step cannot be delegated to a non-lawyer.

Step 4: Add human specifics. This is where the content goes from adequate to excellent. Add real examples from your practice (anonymized appropriately), reference the specific Ontario courts relevant to your clients, mention the actual experience level your lawyers bring to this area, and write in the firm's genuine voice. This is what creates the E-E-A-T signals that AI SEO keyword strategy depends on.

Step 5: SEO pass. Ensure the target keyword appears in the H1, the first 100 words, at least two H2 headings, the meta description, and the image alt text. Check that there are at least five internal links to related content. Verify the canonical URL, structured data, and page title length. This is also the moment to add or refine the FAQ section for structured data purposes.

What Google Thinks About AI-Generated Legal Content

The question law firms ask most often about AI content is the same one: will Google penalize us for using it? The honest answer is nuanced but not complicated.

Google's official position is that it evaluates content based on quality, helpfulness, and expertise — not based on the process used to create it. A page written entirely by a human that is thin, generic, and inaccurate will underperform. A page that started as a Gemini draft but was thoroughly reviewed, corrected, and enriched by an Ontario lawyer can perform very well. The tool used to produce the first draft is not, by itself, a ranking factor.

What does matter is E-E-A-T. Google's quality raters and algorithms are specifically designed to evaluate whether content demonstrates real Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. For law firm content, this means the page needs to demonstrate that it was written or reviewed by someone who actually practices law — ideally in the specific jurisdiction the content covers. Content about Ontario family law should demonstrate knowledge of Ontario courts, Ontario statutes, and Ontario-specific legal practice. Generic content that could apply to any common law jurisdiction will consistently underperform against Ontario-specific content written with genuine expertise.

Content Types Where Gemini Saves the Most Time

Not all content carries equal risk. Some types of law firm content are well-suited to AI assistance; others should remain primarily human-created regardless of how good AI tools become. The distinction is roughly this: content where the primary value is structure and completeness is well-suited to AI. Content where the primary value is specificity, credibility, or emotional resonance should stay human-led.

The distinction matters for AI SEO for law firms specifically because Google's algorithms have become increasingly good at identifying the difference between content that demonstrates real legal expertise and content that was assembled from publicly available training data. Your primary service pages — the pages that need to rank for "family lawyer Toronto" or "criminal defence Mississauga" — should not be AI-first drafts. They should be carefully written by someone who genuinely knows the practice area, then optimized for search.

Where Gemini creates the most value is in supporting content: the blog posts, FAQ articles, and educational pieces that build topical authority around your primary pages. A criminal defence firm in Ontario might publish three supporting articles per month about topics related to their practice — impaired driving charges, possession offences, bail hearings. Gemini can accelerate the first-draft phase of all three while the lawyer review process ensures they're accurate before publication.

Building a Realistic AI Content Process for Your Firm

The firms that get the most value from AI content tools aren't the ones that use Gemini the most aggressively — they're the ones that have built a sustainable, repeatable process that integrates AI into a workflow that still produces high-quality, legally accurate content. Here's what that looks like in practice for an Ontario law firm.

Week 1: Topic identification. The best content topics come from real client questions, not from keyword tools. At the beginning of each month, the lawyer reviews the questions they received from clients during consultations the previous week and identifies four topics that came up repeatedly. These are always the highest-value topics because they reflect what people actually need to know — not what an SEO tool thinks they should want to know. In a family law practice in Hamilton, this might be "What happens to the matrimonial home when we separate?" or "How does the court calculate child support if I'm self-employed?"

Week 2: Gemini drafts all four. Using specific, Ontario-contextualized prompts, the firm generates rough drafts for all four topics. This should take two to three hours total. The output is treated as unverified material that needs significant review — not as finished content. Each draft gets flagged with every legal claim, statute reference, and procedural description that needs to be verified.

Week 3: Lawyer review and rewrite. This is the most important step in the process. The lawyer reviews each flagged item against primary sources, corrects any errors, adds Ontario-specific depth from their actual experience, and rewrites sections that feel generic or that don't reflect how things actually work in Ontario courts. Depending on the lawyer's writing pace, this takes roughly half a day per article — much faster than writing from scratch, but not trivial.

Week 4: SEO pass, publishing, and internal linking. Each article gets its SEO audit: canonical URL, meta description, heading structure, internal links to at least five related pages, structured data, and image alt text. The article is published, and the firm's existing related pages are updated to include a link to the new article. This last step — updating existing pages with links to the new content — is often skipped and is one of the most valuable SEO actions a firm can take after publishing new content.

This process produces four quality articles per month at roughly a third of the cost of outsourcing to a legal content agency and with higher quality control than most agencies provide, because the reviewing lawyer actually practices in the relevant area. Over twelve months, it builds a library of 48 articles that establish genuine topical authority across the firm's practice areas.

The Competitive Advantage
Most Ontario law firms publish fewer than six pieces of content per year. A firm using this AI-assisted process publishes 48 per year — all reviewed for accuracy and optimized for search. The compounding effect of that content over 24 months creates a topical authority advantage that becomes very difficult for competitors to close. The firms doing this now are building moats.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Google Gemini write legal blog posts for my law firm?

Yes, with significant caveats. Gemini can produce rough drafts quickly, but every piece of legal content must be reviewed by a lawyer before publication. Ontario's Rules of Professional Conduct require accuracy in marketing materials, and Gemini has a known tendency to state incorrect legal information with confidence.

Will Google penalize my law firm website for using AI-generated content?

Google's stated position is that it does not penalize content based on how it was created — only on whether it is helpful, accurate, and demonstrates real expertise. AI-generated legal content that hasn't been lawyer-reviewed typically fails these tests, not because it's AI-written, but because it tends to be generic and sometimes factually wrong.

Does Gemini know Canadian law and Ontario-specific legal information?

Gemini has general knowledge of Canadian law but its training data has a cutoff date and it doesn't have real-time access to current Ontario statutes, recent court decisions, or LSO rule updates. Always have an Ontario lawyer verify any jurisdiction-specific claims before publishing.

How should I fact-check Gemini-generated legal content for my firm?

Cross-reference every legal claim against primary sources: the actual Ontario statute (available on ontario.ca), the specific limitation period under the Limitations Act, 2002, and current LSO rules. If Gemini cites a case or statute, verify it exists and says what Gemini claims — hallucinated citations are a common problem.

Is Gemini better than ChatGPT for law firm content writing?

Both tools produce similar quality rough drafts for legal content. Gemini has the advantage of being integrated with Google Search, which can sometimes give it access to more current information. However, for law firm content writing, the tool matters less than the human review process that follows — neither AI should be published without lawyer oversight.
