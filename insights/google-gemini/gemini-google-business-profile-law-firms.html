Google Gemini & Your Google Business Profile

When someone asks Gemini to recommend a lawyer, the first place it looks is your Google Business Profile. Here's what needs to be there — and what will quietly disqualify you.

10 min read
Updated June 2026
LexScale.ai Editorial

Why Gemini Starts With Google Business Profile

Google Business Profile is not just a directory listing. It is the single most authoritative structured data source Google has for any local business — and that means it's also one of the primary inputs Gemini draws on when generating local lawyer recommendations.

When a user types "recommend a family lawyer in Mississauga" into Gemini, the AI doesn't crawl the entire web fresh in that moment. It works from knowledge that's already been indexed and structured. GBP is the richest, most reliable source of that structured local business data in Google's ecosystem. It feeds directly into Google's Knowledge Graph — the massive database of entities, relationships, and facts that powers Google Search, Google Maps, and increasingly, Gemini's conversational responses.

Think of it this way: your website is where your detailed content lives. Your Google Business Profile is where your identity as a local business is confirmed. Gemini needs both, but the GBP data — business name, category, location, services, reviews — is what allows Gemini to understand that you are a specific, real, verified legal services provider in a specific Ontario city. Without a complete and accurate GBP, you may not register as a credible entity in Gemini's local knowledge at all.

KEY INSIGHT
Your Google Business Profile is the front door to Gemini's local recommendations. A law firm with a complete, active, well-reviewed GBP has a structural advantage in Gemini local results over a firm whose GBP is incomplete — regardless of how good their website content is. Both matter, but GBP data is processed differently and often weighted more heavily for local queries.

This is directly relevant to how Gemini finds law firms in local markets. The firms that appear consistently in Gemini's "recommend a lawyer near me" type responses are almost always the firms with the most complete, accurate, and recently updated GBP profiles. That's not a coincidence — it's the system working as designed.

The GBP Fields That Influence Gemini Recommendations

Not all GBP fields are equal. Some have a direct impact on whether Gemini will surface your firm for a given query; others are supporting signals. Here's what actually matters, in order of importance for AI recommendations.

01
Business Name
Must match exactly what appears on the Law Society of Ontario directory. Inconsistency between GBP name and LSO listing creates entity confusion — Gemini may treat them as different businesses.
02
Primary Category
Use "Lawyer" as primary category — it's the broadest signal and catches most general queries. This is more important than any other single field for AI routing.
03
Secondary Categories
Add Family Lawyer, Criminal Justice Attorney, Real Estate Attorney, etc. These allow Gemini to match your listing to specific practice area queries, not just general "lawyer" searches.
04
Business Description
Use all 750 characters. Include primary practice areas, Ontario cities served, and years of experience. Write for a human reader but include the keywords Gemini uses to categorize your practice.
05
Services Section
List every practice area with full sentence descriptions, not just labels. "Family Law" is weaker than "Separation and divorce representation for Ontario clients, including property division, child custody, and support agreements."
06
NAP Consistency
Name, address, and phone number must match exactly across your GBP, website, LSO directory, Justia, Avvo, FindLaw, and Canadian Bar Association listings. Any mismatch weakens entity confidence.
07
Website URL
Link to a specific practice area page rather than your homepage where possible. This signals to Gemini which specific legal services are your primary focus.
08
Photos & Freshness
Exterior photos, interior photos, and lawyer headshots. Google's algorithms — and by extension Gemini — treat photo freshness as an activity signal. Update quarterly at minimum.

The Q&A section is frequently overlooked but worth specific attention. Google allows businesses to add their own questions and answers to the GBP listing — and most law firms leave this section empty, letting random users post questions that may not reflect the firm's practice areas accurately. Proactively adding 8-10 Q&As about your most common client questions signals to Gemini that your listing is actively managed and helps structure how Gemini understands what your firm does. Reviews: The Signal Gemini Weighs Most Heavily

Of all the GBP signals, reviews are the one that most directly influences whether your firm appears in Gemini's local recommendations — and they work in a way that surprises many law firms.

Gemini uses review signals in two distinct dimensions: volume and recency. These are not interchangeable. A firm with 200 reviews from three to five years ago will lose in Gemini recommendations to a firm with 40 reviews from the past eight months. The reason is that recency is a proxy for relevance: Google uses it to signal that the business is active, currently serving clients, and receiving ongoing feedback. A review profile that stopped growing in 2021 suggests a firm that may no longer be as active or relevant.

Ontario context matters here. The Law Society of Ontario's rules do permit client testimonials on Google — you are not required to solicit anonymous reviews. Best practice is to ask clients directly at file close: "If you were happy with the service, a Google review would help other people in similar situations find us." This is a natural, ethically appropriate ask that most clients are willing to fulfill if the work was good.

IMPORTANT
Gemini reads and processes your responses to reviews — not just the reviews themselves. A firm that responds to every review (positive and negative) with a professional, empathetic tone signals to Google that the business is actively managed. A firm that has never responded to any review signals the opposite. Your response tone is visible and it matters: never argue with a negative reviewer, never disclose client information, and always acknowledge the concern.

Building a sustainable review acquisition process is one of the highest-leverage GBP improvements a law firm can make. Set a simple policy: every closed file gets a thank-you email that includes a direct link to your Google review page. Track monthly how many reviews you're receiving. Aim for at least two to three new reviews per month at minimum — this keeps the recency signal strong and prevents your profile from stagnating. This connects directly to local AI SEO for law firms, where review signals are one of the core ranking factors.

When negative reviews appear — and they will, in any active practice — respond within 24 hours. Acknowledge the concern, express that you take client feedback seriously, and offer to connect offline to discuss. Never identify the client or discuss the specifics of their matter. The response is not primarily for the reviewer — it's for every prospective client who reads the review thread afterwards, and for Gemini's assessment of how your firm handles conflict.

How to Optimize Your GBP Specifically for AI Recommendations

General GBP optimization advice and AI-specific GBP optimization advice are not the same thing. Gemini looks at signals that traditional local SEO guidance often underweights. Here are the specific actions that have the most impact on AI recommendation visibility.

Google Posts, published weekly. Gemini has been observed pulling content from recent Google Posts when generating responses about local businesses. A post from last week signals active engagement. A profile with no Posts in six months is a profile that Gemini may treat as stale. Posts don't need to be long — 150-word updates about a recent legal development relevant to your practice area, a seasonal reminder about limitation periods, or a brief firm news update all count. The act of posting matters as much as the content.

Services written as full sentences. Most law firms fill in the Services section with one or two-word labels: "Family Law," "Criminal Defence," "Real Estate." This is weak data. Write each service as a full sentence or short paragraph: "Ontario family law representation including separation agreements, contested divorce, child custody disputes, parenting time arrangements, and spousal support applications for clients across the GTA and Hamilton." This structured-language description is much more useful to Gemini for query matching than a label alone.

Photos tagged and current. Add real photos of your team and office. AI systems cross-reference visual data with other sources — a lawyer headshot on your GBP that matches LinkedIn and your website reinforces entity consistency. Exterior photos help Google confirm your physical location. Interior photos signal that the business is real and operational. Update photos at least quarterly to maintain freshness signals.

Business hours must be accurate. Gemini factors responsiveness and operational status into local recommendations. An incorrect hours listing — especially one that shows a firm as closed when it isn't, or open during holidays when it's not — creates negative signals. Review hours before every major holiday and update them accurately. Use the "Holiday Hours" feature in GBP to manage exceptions rather than leaving the standard hours wrong.

Common GBP Mistakes That Disqualify Law Firms from Gemini Results

Understanding what removes you from Gemini consideration is as important as understanding what gets you included. These are the mistakes that most consistently suppress law firm visibility in Gemini local recommendations.

  • Mismatched business name. If your GBP says "Smith Law" but your website says "Smith & Associates Law Professional Corporation," Gemini may treat these as two separate entities — or fail to confidently identify your firm at all. Your GBP name should match your official LSO-registered business name exactly.
  • Wrong primary category. Lawyers who choose "Legal Services" instead of "Lawyer" as their primary category often underperform in Gemini. "Lawyer" is the category that routes most legal queries. This is worth verifying in your GBP dashboard today.
  • No service descriptions. Labels without descriptions provide minimal data for Gemini's query matching. A GBP Services section with only labels is a significant missed opportunity.
  • Stale photos. A GBP that hasn't had new photos added in two or more years signals an inactive business. Even if the firm is thriving, the absence of fresh photos creates a negative recency signal.
  • No Q&A section activity. An empty or unmanaged Q&A section is a missed ranking signal. Worse, it leaves the door open for irrelevant or misleading questions to appear without answers.
  • Zero review responses. A law firm that has never responded to any review — positive or negative — signals to Gemini that the profile is unmanaged. This directly reduces trust score.
  • Duplicate listings. A firm where three individual lawyers each have their own GBP listings at the same address — all with slightly different business names and categories — creates entity confusion. Gemini may fail to confidently recommend any of them. Consolidate or carefully differentiate.
  • NAP inconsistency across directories. If your firm's name, address, or phone number differs between your GBP, Justia profile, Avvo listing, FindLaw listing, and Canadian Bar Association directory, Gemini's confidence in your entity decreases. Audit all directory listings annually and correct any inconsistencies.

Tracking Whether Your GBP Is Driving Gemini Visibility

One of the frustrating realities of Gemini optimization in 2026 is that there is no direct "Gemini impressions" metric available in GBP Insights yet. Google has not released a tracking tool that shows you specifically how many times your listing was surfaced in a Gemini AI response. But there are reliable proxy signals you can track to understand whether your GBP optimization is improving your overall AI visibility.

Profile Views
Monthly GBP impressions trending up = broader discovery
Direction Requests
Signals high-intent local discovery, often from AI surfaces
Phone Calls
Direct calls from GBP indicate strong recommendation placement
Website Clicks
GBP-to-website traffic is a proxy for Gemini referral activity

The most direct method of testing your Gemini visibility is manual: search for your firm's services in your city directly in Gemini and observe whether you appear. Try queries like "recommend a family lawyer in [your city]," "best criminal defence lawyer [your city]," and "find a real estate lawyer near [your city]." Note whether you appear, where you appear in the list, and what information Gemini presents from your profile. Repeat this test monthly after making GBP updates to track improvement.

If your GBP metrics are flat or declining after three months of active optimization, the issue is often NAP inconsistency across directories, duplicate listings, or a review profile that has stagnated. Audit all three before making further changes to the GBP itself. The broader framework for measuring AI search visibility is covered in detail in our Gemini SEO for lawyers guide.

The Long View
GBP optimization is not a one-time task — it's an ongoing maintenance discipline. The law firms that will consistently appear in Gemini's local recommendations over the next two to three years are the ones treating their GBP as a living asset: updated weekly, responded to within 24 hours, and audited monthly for accuracy. The firms treating it as a set-it-and-forget-it listing will find themselves progressively displaced by competitors who understand how AI recommendation systems actually work.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my Google Business Profile affect how Gemini recommends lawyers?

Yes — significantly. Google Business Profile is one of the primary data sources Gemini draws on for local business recommendations. An incomplete or outdated GBP can quietly disqualify your firm from appearing in Gemini's local lawyer recommendations entirely.

How many Google reviews do I need to appear in Gemini recommendations?

There's no hard threshold, but patterns show that law firms with 20 or more recent reviews (within the last 12 months) appear far more consistently in Gemini local recommendations. Volume matters, but recency matters more — 50 reviews from 4 years ago will underperform compared to 25 reviews from the last year.

Does my GBP primary category affect Gemini recommendations?

Yes, and it's one of the most overlooked factors. Google uses your primary category to route queries to your listing. "Lawyer" is the broadest category and catches most general queries. Add secondary categories for your specific practice areas — "Family Lawyer," "Criminal Justice Attorney," "Real Estate Attorney" — so Gemini can match you to specific practice area queries.

Can a law firm have multiple Google Business Profile listings?

A firm with multiple physical office locations can have one GBP listing per location. Individual lawyers at a firm can also have their own listings, but this can create entity confusion in Gemini if the business name, address, and services aren't carefully differentiated. Duplicate or conflicting listings are one of the most common reasons firms disappear from Gemini recommendations.

How often should I update my Google Business Profile?

At minimum, review your GBP monthly. Add a Google Post at least weekly (case results, legal tips, firm news). Update your photos quarterly. Respond to every review within 48 hours. Verify that all information — hours, phone number, address — is accurate, especially before and after holidays.
