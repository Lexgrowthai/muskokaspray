Google Gemini & Law Firm
Reputation Management

What Gemini knows about your law firm's reputation is pulled from dozens of sources you may not be monitoring. Here's how to take control of what AI search says about you.

How Gemini Builds Its View of Your Firm's Reputation

Google Gemini doesn't have a "reputation score" for law firms stored somewhere in a database. What it has is something more complex and harder to manage: an aggregated model of your firm built from every source it has crawled and indexed. Google reviews, legal directory profiles, news mentions, bar association records, court decisions, client forum discussions, social media mentions, and your own website — all of these contribute to the picture Gemini forms about your law firm.

This matters enormously because it means your reputation in Gemini's eyes is the sum total of your entire digital footprint, not just the content you've deliberately created and published. A single negative news story from three years ago that's still indexed, or a legal directory profile you set up and forgot about, can still be shaping how Gemini describes your firm to prospective clients today.

For an Ontario law firm, the sources Gemini draws from when building its view of your reputation typically include:

Law Society of Ontario Directory
Google Business Profile Reviews
Avvo Profile & Reviews
Justia Profile
CanLII Court Decisions
Toronto Star / Ottawa Citizen
LinkedIn Profiles
Yelp / Local Directories

The breadth of these sources is what makes law firm reputation management in the Gemini era qualitatively different from the Google review management most firms have been doing for the past decade. You're no longer managing a single reputation channel — you're managing a multi-source entity profile that Gemini is constantly re-reading and re-synthesizing. The Signals Gemini Monitors That Most Law Firms Ignore

Most Ontario law firms have gotten the message about Google reviews — they know they should be collecting them regularly and responding professionally. But the reputation signals Gemini uses extend well beyond Google reviews, and many firms are effectively invisible to Gemini on dimensions they don't even know exist.

01
Avvo & Justia Profile Completeness
Gemini indexes these legal directories to build entity understanding. An incomplete or unclaimed profile is a missed authority signal — and a gap Gemini may fill with outdated information.
02
CanLII Case Mentions
If your lawyers appear in Ontario court decisions published on CanLII, that's an authority signal Gemini can read directly. Frequent court mentions correlate with deep practice area expertise.
03
LSO Public Directory Status
Gemini checks whether your lawyers are listed in good standing with the Law Society of Ontario and what practice areas they're authorized for. Accuracy here is critical.
04
Local News Coverage
Coverage in the Toronto Star, Ottawa Citizen, Hamilton Spectator, or local Bar Association newsletters creates third-party authority signals that Gemini weights as external validation.
05
LinkedIn Profiles of Lawyers
Gemini cross-references individual lawyer LinkedIn profiles to understand a firm's composition, seniority, and areas of expertise. Thin profiles weaken the entity signal.
06
Forum & Community Mentions
Reddit, legal advice forums, and community boards can surface informal reputation signals. A firm frequently praised (or criticized) in online communities creates a reputation pattern Gemini can detect.

The practical implication here is that reputation management for the Gemini era requires a wider audit scope than most firms are currently conducting. It's not enough to monitor Google reviews and respond to them — you need to understand and actively manage every signal source Gemini is reading. How Negative Reviews Affect Gemini Recommendations

One of the most common questions Ontario law firms ask about Gemini reputation management is whether negative reviews will cause Gemini to stop recommending them. The answer is nuanced — and understanding it changes how you should be approaching review management entirely.

Negative reviews don't automatically disqualify a firm from Gemini recommendations. What matters more is the overall pattern — the ratio of positive to negative, the recency of the reviews, the volume of review activity, and crucially: how the firm responds to negative reviews.

Gemini has been observed reading review responses to assess a firm's professionalism. This is a significant insight: a dismissive, defensive, or argumentative response to a negative review can damage your Gemini reputation more than the negative review itself. Gemini's language model can assess the tone and content of your responses and use that assessment to form a view of how your firm treats clients when things go wrong.

LSO CAUTION — Professional Conduct & Review Responses
Under Ontario's Rules of Professional Conduct, you cannot reveal confidential client information in a review response — even to defend against a false or misleading claim. This means you cannot respond to a negative review by explaining what actually happened, as this may constitute disclosure of client information. The best approach: acknowledge the concern professionally, express your commitment to client service, and invite the reviewer to contact you directly. Never provide details about the matter, the client, or the outcome.

On the volume dimension: if negative reviews make up more than 15–20% of your recent reviews, that's a pattern Gemini will weight negatively when deciding whether to recommend your firm. Recency also matters — a cluster of negative reviews from two years ago matters far less than negative reviews from the past three months. This is why consistent, ongoing review generation is so important: you want your recent review pattern to be overwhelmingly positive so that any outlier negative reviews are contextually minor.

The practical strategy: (1) Systematically ask recently closed clients for reviews. Even a fraction of your clients leaving reviews creates significant volume over time. (2) Respond to every review — positive and negative — within 48 hours. (3) Treat your review responses as public communications about your firm's values, not just a defence mechanism against criticism.

Managing Your Presence on Legal Directories Gemini Uses

Legal directories are among the most authoritative sources Gemini uses when building its understanding of Ontario law firms. These platforms have established credibility in the legal information space, and Gemini treats data from them as highly reliable. This makes directory management a critical but often neglected element of Gemini reputation strategy.

Ontario-Specific Directories
Law Society of Ontario public directory
Criminal Lawyers' Association
Family Lawyers Association of Ontario
Ontario Bar Association member directory
County and District Law Presidents' Association
National & International Directories
Canadian Bar Association directory
Avvo (claim and complete your profile)
Justia (fill every available field)
FindLaw Canada
Martindale-Hubbell

For each directory, the approach is the same: claim your profile, complete every available field, add your biography and practice area descriptions, ensure your contact details exactly match your Google Business Profile and website, and where reviews are available, actively solicit them from clients.

The entity consistency point deserves particular emphasis. Consistent NAP — Name, Address, Phone number — across all of these directories is critical for Gemini's entity resolution. If your firm is listed as "Smith & Jones Law" on your website, "Smith and Jones LLP" on Avvo, and "Smith, Jones & Associates" on Justia, Gemini may struggle to confirm these are the same entity — and may weight each source's information less confidently as a result. Pick one consistent name format and apply it everywhere.

For the LSO directory specifically: keep it current. The LSO's public lawyer directory is one of the most authoritative sources Gemini uses for Ontario law firms. If a lawyer at your firm has changed practice areas, moved offices, or been called to the bar with an updated certificate number, ensure the LSO directory reflects this. Gemini treats the LSO as a primary trust source — outdated information there undermines your overall entity profile. What to Do When Gemini Says Something Wrong About Your Firm

It happens. Gemini may state outdated information about your firm — an old address, a wrong list of practice areas, incorrect lawyers listed as current members. It may conflate your firm with another firm in your city with a similar name. Or it may surface a news story that, taken out of context or several years after the fact, creates a misleading impression of your firm.

Understanding your options here requires separating two different scenarios: incorrect factual information, and unflattering but accurate information.

For incorrect factual information — wrong address, wrong phone number, practice areas you no longer offer, lawyers who have left the firm — the most effective path is to update all primary sources simultaneously. Update your Google Business Profile. Update your website. Update your Avvo, Justia, and other directory profiles. Update your LSO profile if relevant. When the primary sources Gemini reads all consistently reflect the correct information, Gemini will update its understanding — though this typically takes 4–12 weeks to propagate fully.

For a negative news story that Gemini surfaces prominently: you can create counter-content on your own website. A detailed, professional article addressing the matter — providing context, explaining the outcome, or noting that the situation was resolved — gives Gemini an authoritative first-party source to read alongside the news coverage. This doesn't remove the news story, but it dilutes its impact by providing context Gemini can incorporate into its synthesized understanding of your firm.

For factually incorrect content about your firm that Google has specifically indexed: you can use Google's business information feedback tools to flag the error. This is different from trying to change Gemini's training data — it's an appeal to Google's business data systems that feed Gemini's understanding of specific entities.

Expect A Wait
Gemini doesn't update instantly. Even after you've corrected every primary source, expect 4–12 weeks before those corrections are consistently reflected in Gemini's responses about your firm. Patience is essential — and it's precisely why proactive reputation management is so much more effective than reactive correction. The firms that monitor and maintain their digital presence continuously rarely face a crisis of inaccurate Gemini representation, because the authoritative correct information is always the dominant signal.

If the information Gemini is surfacing about your firm is legally defamatory under Ontario law — false statements of fact that damage your professional reputation — consult with a defamation lawyer about your options. Ontario's defamation law may provide recourse against the original publisher of the content, even if it can't directly reach Gemini's training data. Building a Proactive Reputation Strategy for AI Search

The firms that navigate Gemini reputation management most effectively are not the ones who are best at fixing problems — they're the ones who prevent problems from becoming significant by maintaining an consistently strong and accurate digital presence. Proactive reputation management is dramatically less expensive and stressful than reactive crisis management.

Here's a framework organized by cadence:

Monthly
Monitor & Respond
Check your firm's name in Gemini and note what it says. Review all new GBP reviews and respond within 48 hours. Check for new mentions in local news or forums.
Monthly
Generate Reviews
Systematically request reviews from clients with recently closed files. Even 2–3 new reviews per month creates significant volume and recency signals over a year.
Quarterly
Directory Audit
Check all major directory profiles for accuracy — firm name, address, phone, practice areas, individual lawyer listings. Update anything that has changed.
Quarterly
LinkedIn Review
Review all lawyers' LinkedIn profiles for completeness and accuracy. Add recent cases (where permitted), education, and practice area descriptions.
Annually
Full Footprint Audit
Search your firm name + city across all major platforms. Address any inconsistencies in NAP, outdated content, or platforms where you lack a presence.
Annually
Content & Authority Update
Update your GBP profile photo and business description. Publish a year-in-review post summarizing your firm's work. Refresh key practice area pages.

One often-overlooked element of proactive reputation management: publishing a year-in-review content piece on your website is a particularly effective way to create a fresh, authoritative first-party source for Gemini to read. A post that describes your firm's major accomplishments in the past year — types of cases handled, outcomes achieved (where ethically permissible to discuss), team developments — gives Gemini a recent, positive, and rich source of accurate information about your firm that it can draw from when describing you to prospective clients.

The underlying principle of all proactive reputation management in the Gemini era is this: you can't control what Gemini says about your firm, but you can control the sources Gemini reads. When those sources are consistently positive, accurate, comprehensive, and authoritative, Gemini's representation of your firm will be too. Frequently Asked Questions

Can I control what Google Gemini says about my law firm?

Not directly — you can't tell Gemini what to say. But you can control the sources Gemini reads. By keeping your GBP accurate, your legal directory profiles complete, your website content current, and your reviews recent and positive, you influence what Gemini knows about your firm. The more authoritative and consistent your digital presence, the more accurately Gemini will represent you.

Do negative Google reviews affect how Gemini recommends my firm?

Yes — but not in a simple "bad review = excluded" way. Gemini assesses review patterns: recency, volume, and the content of both reviews and your responses. A firm with a few older negative reviews but many recent positive reviews and professional responses will fare better than a firm with only positive reviews but very few and very old. How you respond to negative reviews is itself a reputation signal Gemini can read.

How does Gemini handle Law Society of Ontario complaints about a lawyer?

The LSO publishes discipline decisions publicly. If a complaint against a lawyer at your firm has resulted in a public LSO discipline decision, Gemini can and does index those decisions. If a lawyer has been suspended, disbarred, or received a public reprimand, that information is part of your firm's digital footprint. The best response is to address the situation directly on your website — transparency and context outperform hoping Gemini won't find it.

Can I request that Gemini update wrong information about my firm?

You cannot directly submit corrections to Gemini's training data. However, you can update the primary sources it reads — your Google Business Profile, your website, your legal directory profiles. For inaccurate information that Google has specifically indexed about your business, you can use Google's business information feedback tools. For content that is legally defamatory under Ontario law, consult with a defamation lawyer about your options.

What legal directories does Gemini use when researching Ontario law firms?

Based on observed citation patterns, Gemini draws heavily on: the Law Society of Ontario's public lawyer directory, Avvo, Justia, FindLaw, and the Canadian Bar Association directory. It also indexes practice-specific associations (Criminal Lawyers' Association, Family Lawyers Association of Ontario), CanLII court decisions that mention specific lawyers and firms, local news sources (Toronto Star, Ottawa Citizen, local bar newsletters), and LinkedIn profiles of individual lawyers.
